[1] Should they stay or should they go?: Effects of Changes in College Admission Policies.

[2] It's Always Sunny in Politics. (with [JJ Naddeo](https://sites.google.com/georgetown.edu/jjnaddeo))

<details>
<summary>Abstract</summary>
<p>
How do citizens decide who to vote for? In democracies where voting is not mandatory one must first answer how citizens decide if they should vote at all. This paper leverages daily measurements of weather conditions from satellites, merged to 68 years of US Presidential elections, to offer insight into both stages of the voting process. We show that while precipitation plays a role in a voter's decision to vote, sunshine plays a large role once they have ballot in hand. The mechanism follows from two stylized facts. The first links increased sunshine to boosts in mood, and the second shows that heightened mood increases a persons willingness to take risks. In this paper we show that an increase in sunshine is enough to sway undecided voters, in non-competitive elections, to vote for the ex-ante riskier candidates. 

</p>
