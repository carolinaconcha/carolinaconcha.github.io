## Work in Progress

[1] Should they stay or should they go?: Effects of Changes in College Admission Policies.

<details>
<summary>Abstract</summary>
<p>
  
This paper studies the connection between high school students' decisions to switch schools and admission to college policies by exploiting the inclusion of students' within-school grade point (GPA) ranking in Chile. The goal of this policy was to increase admission rates of both segregated minorities and high-performance individuals by incorporating a bonus to the GPA score, producing incentives for students to relocate to schools with weaker academic competition. I present a theoretical model to understand who has incentives to arbitrage the policy by switching high schools in 12th grade. Empirically, using a Event Study and Difference-in-Difference approach, I show that the policy successfully increased low-tier schools students’ likelihood of going to college, but that this effect disappeared after students from higher-tier schools strategically relocated schools. I also find evidence that these strategic movers have a marked increase in their application scores after switching schools.

</p>

[2] It's Always Sunny in Politics. (with [JJ Naddeo](https://sites.google.com/georgetown.edu/jjnaddeo))

<details>
<summary>Abstract</summary>
<p>
How do citizens decide who to vote for? In democracies where voting is not mandatory one must first answer how citizens decide if they should vote at all. This paper leverages daily measurements of weather conditions from satellites, merged to 68 years of US Presidential elections, to offer insight into both stages of the voting process. We show that while precipitation, a proxy for cost of voting, plays a role in a voter's decision to vote, sunshine plays a large role once they have ballot in hand. The explanation for sunshine's impact follows from two stylized facts. The first links increased sunshine to boosts in mood, and the second shows that heightened mood increases a persons willingness to take risks. In this paper we show that an increase in sunshine is enough to sway undecided voters to vote for the ex-ante riskier candidates. We also show that precipitation does little to affect electoral outcomes and uniformly depresses turnout. 

</p>
</details>
