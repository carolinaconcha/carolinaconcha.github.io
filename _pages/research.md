## Work in Progress

[1] Should They Stay or Should They Go? How High School Students Respond to Changes in College Admissions Policies.

<details>
<summary>Abstract</summary>
<p>
  
This paper studies high school students’ reallocation decision when admission to college policies change by exploiting the inclusion of students’ within-school grade point (GPA) ranking in Chile. While the goal of this policy is to increase admission rates of both segregated minorities and high-performance students, the incorporation of a school-specific bonus to students’ GPA in high school could have unintended consequences. I construct a theoretical model to understand which students have motive and opportunity for strategic high school choice in 12th grade. My main model’s prediction suggests students with high school performance neither on the top nor at the bottom of the grade distribution have incentives to reallocate to schools with weaker academic competition. Empirically, using an Event Study approach, I show students who switched schools are from top public schools, and they tend to target high schools with lower average GPA and fewer number students going to college; these strategic movers have a marked increase in their application scores. The results suggest that strategic behavior may be important when constructing policies to increase admission rates for disadvantaged students based on high school performance.

</p>
</details>  

[2] It's Always Sunny in Politics. (with [JJ Naddeo](https://sites.google.com/georgetown.edu/jjnaddeo))

<details>
<summary>Abstract</summary>
<p>
How do citizens make decisions on election day? In democracies where voting is not mandatory one must first answer how citizens decide if they should vote at all. This paper leverages daily measurements of weather conditions from satellites, merged to 68 years of US Presidential elections, to offer insight into both stages of the voting process. We show that while precipitation, a proxy for cost of voting, plays a role in a voter's decision to vote, sunshine plays a large role once they have ballot in hand. The explanation for sunshine's impact follows from two stylized facts. The first links increased sunshine to boosts in mood, and the second shows that heightened mood increases a person's willingness to take risks. In this paper we show that an increase in sunshine is enough to sway undecided voters to vote for ex-ante riskier candidates. We also show that precipitation does little to affect electoral outcomes and uniformly depresses turnout.

</p>
</details>
